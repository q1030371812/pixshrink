Why compress JPEGs in the browser?

JPEG is the default output for every phone, mirrorless camera, and screenshot tool on the web. The format is already lossy, but most cameras and software export at quality 90+ with extra metadata (EXIF, XMP, ICC profiles) that bloats files by 20–100 KB each. Re-encoding with a modern encoder like mozjpeg removes that overhead and uses better quantization tables, so the same visible quality needs fewer bytes.

Pixshrink runs mozjpeg as WebAssembly directly in your tab. The file you select never leaves your device — no upload, no server queue, no signup wall. You can compress a folder of wedding photos on a flight, or shrink 200 product shots for Shopify, and nothing is logged anywhere.

What size reduction should I expect?

Results depend on the source, but on a typical 4032×3024 phone photo at original quality 95, the mozjpeg pipeline at quality 75 produces a file 80–90% smaller with no visible difference in side-by-side viewing. If you push to quality 60, you can hit 95% reduction; below that, JPEG's 8×8 DCT blocks start to show as ringing around high-contrast edges.

The slider is the main control. A few practical rules of thumb: quality 80 is the sweet spot for hero photos on a website; quality 70 is fine for product thumbnails and avatars; quality 50 works for preview images that load before a higher-res version.

What about EXIF and metadata?

By default Pixshrink strips EXIF (camera, GPS, timestamps) from JPEGs. That alone usually saves 30–80 KB and removes the privacy side-effect of shipping a photo with your home address baked into the file. You can re-enable metadata preservation from the options panel if you need it for archival work.

Also try the other formats

If you want the smallest possible file at the same visual quality, re-encoding to WebP or AVIF usually beats JPEG by another 20–40%. For screenshots and UI captures with sharp text and flat colors, PNG is often already optimal and can be losslessly shrunk with oxipng. For animations, see GIF compression.

Frequently asked questions

Is this JPEG compressor really free?

Yes — no signup, no trial, no daily limit, no watermark. The encoder runs in your browser, so there is no server cost to pass on.

Do my photos get uploaded somewhere?

No. The file is read into memory, processed by mozjpeg compiled to WebAssembly, and the result is offered as a download. Network activity during compression is zero. You can verify this by opening DevTools → Network and watching the panel stay empty.

Will compression reduce image quality?

JPEG is lossy by design, so every re-encode loses some information. The quality slider controls how much. At 75–80 the loss is invisible to most viewers; below 50 you may see ringing and banding on smooth gradients.

Can I batch compress many JPEGs at once?

Yes. Drop a folder onto the page, adjust quality, and Pixshrink will encode everything and download a single ZIP. There is no file count limit, but very large batches are constrained by available RAM.
